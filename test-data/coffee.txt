## Espresso

Espresso is a concentrated coffee brewed by forcing hot water under high pressure through finely-ground coffee beans. It originated in Italy in the early 20th century and has since become the foundation of many popular coffee drinks worldwide. A single shot of espresso is typically 25-30ml and is known for its rich, bold flavor and the thin layer of crema on top. The brewing process takes about 25-30 seconds and requires water at approximately 90-96 degrees Celsius.

## Cappuccino

A cappuccino is a classic Italian coffee drink made with equal parts espresso, steamed milk, and milk foam. The name comes from the Capuchin friars, whose brown robes resembled the color of the drink. In Italy, cappuccino is traditionally consumed only in the morning and never after a meal — ordering one after lunch is considered a cultural faux pas. The ideal cappuccino has a velvety texture with the foam forming a dome shape above the rim of the cup.

## Caffè Americano

An Americano is made by diluting a shot of espresso with hot water, producing a drink similar in strength to drip coffee but with a different flavor profile. It was popularized by American soldiers stationed in Italy during World War II who found traditional espresso too strong and intense for their taste. They would add hot water to dilute the espresso, creating a milder beverage closer to what they were used to drinking back home. Today the Americano is served in cafes around the world and can be enjoyed hot or iced.

## Moka Pot

The Moka pot was invented by Alfonso Bialetti in 1933 and quickly became an icon of Italian home coffee culture. It brews coffee by passing boiling water pressurized by steam through ground coffee, producing a strong and aromatic brew. Nearly every Italian household owns at least one Moka pot, and it remains the most popular way to make coffee at home in Italy. The distinctive octagonal design of the Bialetti Moka Express is recognized worldwide and has been exhibited in museums of modern art.

## Cold Brew

Cold brew coffee is made by steeping coarsely ground coffee in cold or room-temperature water for an extended period, usually 12 to 24 hours. The slow extraction process produces a smooth, less acidic concentrate that can be served over ice or diluted with water or milk. Cold brew became widely popular in specialty coffee shops during the 2010s, though the method of cold-steeping coffee has been practiced in various forms for centuries, particularly in Japan where it is known as Kyoto-style slow drip coffee.
